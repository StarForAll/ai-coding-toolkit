# Customer-Facing PRD Checklist

* Language and expression: the document uses simple, non-technical language that business stakeholders can understand.
* Language and expression: technical terms, acronyms, and specialized vocabulary are defined when first introduced.
* Language and expression: internal system names, technical shorthand, and engineering terminology are avoided or translated.
* Language and expression: requirements are written from the customer's point of view and describe experiences and outcomes.
* Document structure: the document clearly describes the problem being solved or opportunity being addressed.
* Document structure: the document identifies the primary users, secondary users, and stakeholders.
* Document structure: the document states measurable business objectives and expected outcomes.
* Document structure: the document clearly separates core features, enhancements, and out-of-scope items.
* Document structure: the document provides a high-level solution description without technical implementation details.
* Document structure: the document describes key user scenarios and requirements in customer terms.
* Document structure: the document outlines phasing and prioritization of features.
* Document structure: the document defines measurable success metrics from the customer perspective.
* Document structure: the document records assumptions, dependencies, and pending decisions.
* Content quality: each requirement explains why it matters to the customer or business.
* Content quality: requirements focus on business outcomes and customer experiences.
* Content quality: success criteria can be objectively measured and verified.
* Content quality: no critical requirement or assumption is missing.
* Scope boundary: core commitments for the current phase are clearly defined.
* Scope boundary: future enhancements are identified and separated from core deliverables.
* Scope boundary: excluded items are listed with rationale.
* Scope boundary: the scope-change process is documented when it affects stakeholder expectations.
* Validation methods: success criteria use quantifiable metrics where possible.
* Validation methods: success can be observed through customer behavior or business metrics.
* Validation methods: methods for validating success criteria are described.
* Validation methods: clear thresholds for what constitutes success are defined.
* Examples and documentation quality: examples, diagrams, or mockups illustrate requirements without introducing hidden scope.
* Examples and documentation quality: examples do not imply additional features or commitments beyond what is explicitly stated.
* Examples and documentation quality: terminology and definitions stay consistent throughout the document.
* Examples and documentation quality: the document shows evidence of stakeholder review or feedback incorporation.
* Prohibited content: the document does not contain API specifications, database schemas, class designs, or deployment details.
* Prohibited content: internal system names and technical architecture references are avoided or translated.
* Prohibited content: technical concepts are translated into business-friendly descriptions.
